The Amiga Blitter Explained

The Amiga Blitter (short for Block Image Transfer) is a custom hardware unit inside the Amiga’s Agnus chip. Its purpose was to move, combine, and manipulate blocks of graphics data very quickly, freeing the CPU for other tasks.

Why was it important?

In the mid-1980s, most computers used the CPU for every single pixel operation. The Amiga’s blitter allowed smooth scrolling, fast sprite drawing, and complex visual effects. This was one of the reasons why Amiga graphics looked far ahead of its competitors like the Atari ST or early PCs.

What could it do?

  • Block Copy (BitBLT): Copy a rectangle of pixels from one memory area to another.
  • Masked Graphics: Apply logical operations to combine images with transparency.
  • Area Fill: Fill shapes with colors or patterns.
  • Line Drawing: Draw straight lines using hardware (Bresenham’s algorithm).
  • Scrolling: Move large sections of the screen memory efficiently.

How did it work?

The blitter uses four channels, called A, B, C, D:

  • A: Usually the sprite data.
  • B: A mask (defining transparency).
  • C: The background (existing screen pixels).
  • D: The destination (output result).

The programmer configures the blitter through registers such as BLTCON0, BLTxPTH/BLTxPTL, and BLTSIZE. Once set up, the blitter performs the operation without CPU involvement.

Example: Drawing a masked sprite

The blitter can combine channels with boolean logic. A common formula is:

D = (C & ~B) | (A & B)

This means: keep the background where the mask is empty, and draw the sprite where the mask is filled.

Technical Notes

  • It worked with bitplanes, not chunky pixels (each bitplane was 1 bit deep).
  • It processed 16-bit words at a time.
  • Up to 256 possible boolean functions were supported.
  • It shared memory bandwidth with the CPU, but worked independently once triggered.

Impact

The Amiga blitter enabled effects like parallax scrolling, fast sprite animation, and 2D graphics that were unmatched in home computers of its era. Alongside the Copper (another custom co-processor), it made the Amiga a legendary machine in gaming and the demoscene.
